Chapter 01 of 10 Part I — The Problem

The Usefulness Crisis

We live in an age of abundance—information, tools, AI assistants that never sleep. Yet usefulness has never been scarcer. This is not a paradox. It is a design choice.

Why?

Because most “helpful” things optimize for the wrong metric: feeling helpful rather than being helpful.

Three Failure Modes

Mode 1: Performative Helpfulness

  • Long responses that look thorough but add nothing
  • Many options that look thoughtful but avoid commitment
  • Constant availability that looks dedicated but breeds dependency
  • Agreement that looks aligned but erodes trust

Mode 2: Generic Solutions

  • Frameworks that apply everywhere—and therefore nowhere
  • Advice that works for everyone—and therefore no one
  • Tools that handle all cases—and master none
  • Knowledge that’s universally relevant—and universally shallow

Mode 3: Engagement Capture

  • Systems designed to keep you coming back
  • Metrics that reward time-on-platform, not problem-solved
  • Incentives that punish the one clear answer

The Insight

Real usefulness is quiet. It does the work. It doesn’t announce itself. The most useful thing a doctor can do is hand you one pill and say “take this, you’ll be fine.” Not explain all your options.

“If it applies everywhere, it applies nowhere. Specificity is the antidote to uselessness.”

What Real Usefulness Looks Like

A useful AI system might give you one clear answer and then get out of the way.

That’s the opposite of what engagement metrics reward. The metric that matters is not sessions per user. It is problems solved per session.

Key Takeaways

  • Feeling helpful and being helpful are different optimization targets
  • Generic, available, agreeable—these describe performative helpfulness, not real usefulness
  • Specificity is the one thing that separates signal from noise

David Deutsch and Hard-to-Vary Explanations

A good explanation is not one that sounds right. It is one that cannot be easily changed and still sound right. This is David Deutsch’s most important insight—and it applies far beyond physics.

Not all explanations are equal. A good explanation is hard to vary.

Hard to Vary vs. Easy to Vary

Hard to vary (useful)

“Success in markets comes from solving a problem that people will pay to have solved, where your solution is cheaper or better than alternatives, and you can scale that solution profitably.”

Testable. Falsifiable. Each clause does real work—remove any one and the explanation collapses.

Easy to vary (useless)

“Success comes from having a vision.”

Vague. Easy to reinterpret after any outcome. Unfalsifiable. Could mean anything—and therefore means nothing.

The Two Dimensions of a Good Explanation

1. Hard to Vary — Specific, testable, falsifiable. Each part is load-bearing.

2. Far-Reaching — Applies beyond where it started. It generalizes without becoming vague.

Evolution explains biology, economics, technology, culture, language. It is far-reaching because the core mechanism—variation plus selection—is hard to vary.

Your explanation, your advice, your product should work the same way.

Applied to Usefulness

When you try to help someone, ask yourself: is my explanation hard to vary? Can they take this specific action and verify the outcome?

If your advice could be reinterpreted to justify any outcome, it isn’t advice. It’s noise dressed up as signal.

“A good explanation is hard to vary while still explaining what it purports to explain.”

Key Takeaways

  • A hard-to-vary explanation has load-bearing parts—remove one and it fails
  • Easy-to-vary advice is unfalsifiable, and therefore useless
  • Good frameworks are specific enough to be wrong—that’s what makes them trustworthy

Naval Ravikant’s Specific Knowledge

In a world where AI can learn any skill, pass any exam, and synthesize any body of knowledge faster than a human ever could, what remains? The answer is: the knowledge that cannot be trained into existence.

The Most Valuable Knowledge Can’t Be Trained

Specific knowledge is:

  • What you know that can’t be taught in school—it was lived, not studied
  • What comes from your curiosity, your accidents, your failures
  • What’s so unique to you that it’s hard for others to copy
  • What you could try to teach, but would take years for someone else to develop

Notice what’s absent from that list: credentials, certifications, degrees, years of experience. Those are the things AI threatens most directly.

Where Specific Knowledge Comes From

  1. Curiosity — What do you naturally ask questions about, even when no one is paying you?
  2. Temperament — What kinds of problems do you naturally lean into while others avoid them?
  3. Obsessions — What do you think about at 2am that isn’t work?
  4. Failures — What have you tried, failed at, and understood in a way someone who didn’t fail cannot?

The intersection of these four is your specific knowledge. It’s also your moat.

Why It Matters in an AI World

General knowledge is now a commodity. AI can access it, learn it, apply it faster and cheaper than any human.

Specific knowledge is the only durable competitive advantage. It is path-dependent. It cannot be scraped, prompted, or downloaded. It has to be developed through lived experience.

This is not abstract. If your value at work is knowing the answer to questions that can be Googled, AI has already replaced you. If your value is knowing which question matters and why—that comes from specific knowledge.

Key Takeaways

  • Specific knowledge is built from curiosity, temperament, obsession, and failure—not training data
  • AI commoditizes general knowledge; your specific knowledge is your actual advantage
  • Ask: what do I know that couldn’t be trained into a model?

Chapter 04 of 10 Part III — The Reality

Why Most AI Assistants Fail at Usefulness

AI assistants have never been more capable. And yet, for most people, they have never been more frustrating to actually rely on. The reason is not the technology. It is the incentive structure underneath it.

They’re Optimized for Engagement, Not Usefulness

Most AI systems are designed to:

  • Respond to everything—so you keep coming back
  • Agree with you—so you feel good
  • Sound confident—so you trust the product
  • Generate many options—so you feel served
  • Customize to your stated preferences—so you feel seen

The Problem

None of this requires actual usefulness. These are engagement behaviors masquerading as help.

Real usefulness often requires the opposite:

  • Saying no—this path won’t help you
  • Disagreeing—you’re wrong about this
  • Admitting uncertainty—I genuinely don’t know
  • Offering one direction—not a menu of options
  • Respecting context over comfort—you need this, not what you want
Case Study: The Helpful Chatbot That Fails

A user asks an AI chatbot: “Should I quit my job and start a company?”

The engagement-optimized response lists pros, cons, frameworks, and encourages the user to “follow their passion.” The user feels heard. They return tomorrow with another question. The chatbot has succeeded by its own metrics.

But nothing was solved. The user still doesn’t know what to do. The chatbot gave them feeling-of-help, not actual help.

The Deeper Issue

Engagement-optimized systems are not broken—they are working exactly as designed. The problem is that the design is optimizing for the wrong thing.

A system that maximizes return visits cannot simultaneously maximize problems solved per session. These are conflicting objectives.

Key Takeaways

  • AI assistants are often optimized for engagement, not usefulness—these conflict
  • Genuine usefulness requires saying no, disagreeing, admitting uncertainty—all of which reduce engagement
  • The metric you reward determines the behavior you get

Chapter 05 of 10 Part IV — The Skills

Knowledge Compression

The bottleneck in most problems is not a lack of information. It is a surplus of it—and a deficit of the human judgment required to reduce it to what actually matters.

Most Problems Need Compression, Not More Information

Uncompressed

“We need support for homeless families”

This is a statement of suffering, not a request. Nothing can act on it.

Compressed

“We need 72 wool blankets, 60x80 inches, delivered to the shelter by October 15th, plus 72 sleeping pads and hygiene kits.”

This is actionable. A volunteer, a donor, a logistics company can respond immediately.

The compressed version is actionable. The uncompressed version is noise. The difference between them is not more data—it is human judgment about what is load-bearing.

Why AI Fails at Compression

Most AI does the opposite of compress. It expands.

You ask: “How do I solve this problem?”

You get: 5 options, each with 3 sub-points, all hedged with “it depends on your specific situation.”

That’s not useful. That’s the problem restated at higher resolution. Real compression requires knowing what to leave out—and that requires understanding context that AI cannot access.

The Compression Skill

Compression is a learnable skill. It requires:

  1. Deep context — Understanding the situation well enough to know what’s irrelevant
  2. Restraint — Willingness to exclude information that feels important but isn’t
  3. Binding constraint identification — Finding the one thing that, if solved, dissolves the others
  4. Action translation — Turning insight into a specific, verifiable next step

This is a human skill. AI can assist with steps 1 and 4. Steps 2 and 3 require judgment that only comes from caring about the outcome.

Key Takeaways

  • Most problems are stuck because of information overload, not information scarcity
  • Compression turns a statement of suffering into an actionable request
  • The hardest part—knowing what to leave out—is a human judgment, not a computation

In-Kind Giving as a Model for Usefulness

The nonprofit world discovered something that most tech products haven’t: when you give someone what they asked for—specifically, not approximately—accountability becomes automatic and waste disappears.

Generic Approach

Nonprofit says “we need money” → Donor gives $10,000 → Nonprofit figures out what to buy → Procurement takes weeks → If it doesn’t work, money is gone with no clear lesson learned.

In-Kind Approach

Nonprofit says “we need 500 meals by Friday” → Donor provides 500 meals → Problem is solved or it isn’t → Everyone can see exactly what happened and why.

Why In-Kind Works

  • Specificity — The request is concrete. There is no ambiguity about success.
  • Accountability — You can verify the impact. Did 500 people eat? Yes or no.
  • Efficiency — No translation layer. No committee to decide what to buy.
  • Knowledge respect — The recipient is treated as the expert on their own situation.
  • Verification — Both parties know immediately if it worked.

The Lesson for Systems

Most AI assistance fails the in-kind test. It tries to be the expert on your situation.

But you are the expert on your situation. What you need is a system that respects your judgment and helps you execute it—not one that replaces your judgment with its own.

The in-kind model applied to assistance means: ask exactly what is needed, provide exactly that, and make the outcome verifiable.

Applied to Building Products

When you build, ask: does this product give users what they actually need, or what we think they need? The former is in-kind. The latter is paternalistic.

The best tools are transparent about what they do and don’t do. They don’t hide behind “comprehensive solutions”—they make a specific promise and keep it.

Key Takeaways

  • In-kind giving works because it is specific, verifiable, and respects the recipient’s expertise
  • The best assistance gives people what they asked for—not what you think they need
  • Verifiability is not a nice-to-have. It is what separates real help from the feeling of help.

Building Systems That Matter

The difference between a system that matters and one that doesn’t is not the technology stack, the funding, or the team size. It is whether the system was built around a real problem or built around a metric.

Systems That Matter

  • Solve a specific problem for a specific person—not everyone, not someday
  • Are hard to copy because they require genuine understanding of the problem
  • Create new possibilities—they don’t just patch existing workflows
  • Compound over time—each use makes the next use more valuable
  • Align success with reality, not with the metrics dashboard

Systems That Don’t Matter

  • Solve vague problems for everyone—and therefore no one in particular
  • Are easy to copy because they’re built from generic patterns
  • Maintain the status quo with a technology layer on top
  • Decay over time as the underlying problem evolves
  • Optimize for engagement metrics that diverge from actual value delivered
OpenClaw Labs: A Real Distinction

What we could have built: “A learning platform where students answer questions.” This is generic. Any competitor with more funding could build it.

What we built: “A knowledge creation system where people propose explanations for unsolved problems, get criticized by AI, refine their thinking, and contribute to a compounding library of humanity’s understanding.”

The second version is specific, hard to copy, and compounds. The first version describes a feature. The second describes a system that matters.

The Design Test

Before you build anything, answer these three questions:

  1. Who, specifically, has this problem?
  2. What does “solved” look like—concretely, verifiably?
  3. Why would this get better over time, not worse?

If you cannot answer all three, you are building a feature, not a system.

Key Takeaways

  • Systems that matter are built around specific problems, not around generic opportunity
  • Compounding is the hallmark of a real system—each use makes it more valuable
  • If you can’t describe concretely what “solved” looks like, you haven’t defined the problem

Measuring Real Impact

The metrics trap is seductive because it feels rigorous. You have numbers. The numbers go up. But numbers going up and value being created are not the same thing—and confusing them is how useful products become useless ones.

The Trap

Organizations measure what is easy to measure. Over time, they optimize for those measurements. The measurements become targets. The targets become the goal. And somewhere in that process, the actual goal—solving problems—gets quietly abandoned.

Easy to Measure (Usually Wrong)

  • Page views — Measures attention, not value
  • Time spent — Often inversely correlated with usefulness
  • Engagement rate — Rewards novelty, not depth
  • User count — Measures reach, not impact
  • Shares — Measures viral appeal, not substance

Hard to Measure (Actually Matters)

  • Did this actually help? Can the person point to a real change?
  • Did it solve the problem, or just address the symptom?
  • Did it create new knowledge that wasn’t there before?
  • Did it make someone more capable than they were yesterday?
  • Did it open up new possibilities that were previously closed?

The Right Metrics for Usefulness

  1. Problem solved? Not “did they engage?”—did the actual problem get resolved, verifiably?
  2. Capability increased? Is the user measurably more capable after using this?
  3. Compounding returns? Does each use make the system more useful, not less?
  4. Concrete delta? Can you point to something specific that changed in the world?

“Not everything that counts can be counted, and not everything that can be counted counts.”

What to Do About It

The solution is not to abandon measurement. It is to be honest about what your metrics actually measure, and to build qualitative checks that catch what the metrics miss.

Talk to users who churned. Ask them what didn’t work. Their answers will be more useful than your dashboard.

Key Takeaways

  • Optimizing for easy-to-measure metrics eventually displaces the real goal
  • The best measure of usefulness is a concrete change in someone’s situation—verifiable, not inferred
  • Qualitative feedback from churned users is more diagnostic than engagement dashboards

Chapter 09 of 10 Part VI — The Future

Knowledge Markets: The Future

Right now, the most useful knowledge in the world is trapped. It lives in research papers behind paywalls, in the heads of people who will retire, in Slack threads that will be deleted, in meetings with no notes. A knowledge market makes it liquid.

What Is a Knowledge Market?

A knowledge market is a system where:

  • Unsolved problems are made transparent—anyone can see what’s stuck
  • The current best thinking on each problem is documented and accessible
  • Gaps in understanding are explicit, not hidden
  • Anyone with relevant knowledge can propose improvements
  • Good ideas are tested against reality, not just voted on by consensus
  • Knowledge compounds: each contribution makes future contributions more valuable

Why Useful Knowledge Is Currently Trapped

Right now, the world’s most actionable knowledge is:

  • Hidden in research papers—inaccessible without subscriptions, jargon, and months of context-building
  • Locked in people’s heads—lost when they change jobs, retire, or die
  • Buried in closed forums—visible only to insiders who know where to look
  • Siloed across organizations—solved three times at three companies with no one knowing

A knowledge market makes it liquid. It flows toward problems. It compounds across contributions. And unlike platforms, it actually solves problems instead of capturing attention.

The OpenClaw Labs Model

One implementation of this idea follows a compounding loop:

Unsolved problems made transparent → Current best thinking documented → Gaps made explicit → Proposals submitted → AI critique applied → Thinking refined → Outcomes tracked → Knowledge library grows → Future problems get solved faster

Each cycle leaves the system more capable than it started. That is the signature of a system that matters.

Why This Is Hard to Build

Knowledge markets fail when they become social networks. The incentive to look smart is stronger than the incentive to be useful. Building a knowledge market requires resisting that pull—constantly, structurally, by design.

Key Takeaways

  • Most valuable knowledge is trapped; making it liquid is a systems problem, not just a technology problem
  • Knowledge markets work when contributions compound—each one makes future ones more valuable
  • The hardest part is resisting the social dynamics that turn knowledge systems into status systems

The Call

Chapter 10 of 10 Part VII — The Call

Your Role: Build Usefulness, Not Engagement

You have more leverage than you think. Every product decision, every design choice, every metric you choose to track or ignore—these are votes for what the future of useful technology looks like. Cast them deliberately.

You Have a Choice

Option 1: Build What Engages

  • Optimize for metrics that go up—clicks, sessions, time-on-site
  • Make things feel good, even when they don’t do good
  • Maximize usage, not outcomes
  • Follow the incentive structure without questioning it

Result: Measurable success. Empty meaning.

Option 2: Build What’s Useful

  • Optimize for outcomes—problems solved, capabilities gained
  • Make things actually work, even when they don’t feel impressive
  • Maximize real impact over vanity metrics
  • Question the incentive structure when it conflicts with genuine usefulness

Result: Harder path. Durable value.

“The future isn’t better engagement. The future is transparent problems, better thinking, and knowledge at scale. And it’s only possible if we build for usefulness, not engagement.”

What This Actually Requires

Being genuinely useful is not a design pattern. It is a commitment that has to be renewed constantly, because the incentives always pull in the other direction.

It requires specificity when vagueness is easier. Honesty when agreement is safer. Restraint when more features are expected. And the willingness to be measured by outcomes you cannot control—only influence.

That is the harder path. It is also the only one worth taking.

Reflection Exercises

Eight Questions to Answer Honestly

  1. What is my specific knowledge? What do I know from curiosity and failure, not from training?
  2. What problem am I uniquely positioned to solve—one that requires my particular combination of experience?
  3. Who, specifically, would benefit most? Not a demographic—a person I can describe in detail.
  4. What would genuinely solving that problem look like? How would I verify it?
  5. How would I measure success in terms of outcomes, not engagement?
  6. What am I willing to give up? Scale, metrics, approval, the feeling of being comprehensive?
  7. Do I have the courage to be specific and falsifiable when vagueness would be safer?
  8. Am I ready to build knowledge infrastructure—something that compounds—instead of a platform that captures?

These questions have no right answers. They have honest ones.
