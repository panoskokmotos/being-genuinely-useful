Part I: The Problem

The Usefulness Crisis

We live in an age of abundance — information, tools, AI assistants. Yet usefulness has never been scarcer.

Why?

Because most "helpful" things optimize for the wrong metric: feeling helpful rather than being helpful.

Three Failure Modes

Mode 1: Performative Helpfulness

  • Long responses (looks thorough)
  • Many options (looks thoughtful)
  • Constant availability (looks dedicated)
  • Agreement (looks aligned)

Mode 2: Generic Solutions

  • Frameworks that apply everywhere
  • Advice that works for everyone
  • Tools that handle all cases
  • Knowledge that's universally relevant

The Insight

Real usefulness is quiet. It does the work. It doesn't announce itself.

"If it applies everywhere, it applies nowhere. Specificity is the antidote to uselessness."

What Real Usefulness Looks Like

A useful AI system might give you one clear answer and then get out of the way.

That's the opposite of what engagement metrics reward.

Part II: The Framework

David Deutsch and Hard-to-Vary Explanations

David Deutsch discovered something crucial about knowledge:

Not all explanations are equal. A good explanation is hard to vary.

Hard to Vary vs. Easy to Vary

Hard to vary (useful):
"Success in markets comes from solving a problem that people will pay to have solved, where your solution is cheaper or better than alternatives, and you can scale that solution profitably."

Why? Testable components. Falsifiable. Hard to reinterpret.
Easy to vary (useless):
"Success comes from having a vision."

Why? Vague. Easy to reinterpret. Unfalsifiable.

The Two Dimensions

1. Hard to Vary — Specific, testable, falsifiable

2. Far-Reaching — Applies beyond where it started

Evolution explains biology, economics, technology, culture, language. That's far-reaching.

Your explanation should work the same way.

Naval Ravikant's Specific Knowledge

The Most Valuable Knowledge Can't Be Trained

Specific knowledge is:

  • What you know that can't be taught in school
  • What comes from your curiosity, accidents, failures
  • What's so unique to you that it's hard for others to copy
  • What you could teach, but it would take years for someone else to develop

Where It Comes From

  1. Curiosity — What do you naturally ask questions about?
  2. Temperament — What kind of problems do you naturally approach?
  3. Obsessions — What do you think about when nobody's paying you?
  4. Failures — What have you tried, failed at, and learned from?

The intersection of these four is your specific knowledge.

Why It Matters in an AI World

General knowledge is obsolete. AI can look it up, learn it, apply it better.

Specific knowledge is the only competitive advantage. That's what humans can do that machines can't.

Why Most AI Assistants Fail at Usefulness

They're Optimized for Engagement, Not Usefulness

Most AI systems are designed to:

  • Respond to everything (you keep using them)
  • Agree with you (you feel good)
  • Sound confident (you trust them)
  • Generate many options (you feel choice)
  • Customize to preferences (you feel seen)

The Problem

None of this requires actual usefulness.

Real usefulness often requires the opposite:

  • Saying no (this won't help)
  • Disagreeing (you're wrong)
  • Admitting uncertainty (I don't know)
  • Offering one direction (not options)
  • Respecting context over preferences (you need this, not want this)
The Helpful Chatbot That Fails
Users love it. Engagement metrics are great. But does it solve problems? Usually not.

Knowledge Compression

Most Problems Don't Need More Information. They Need Better Compression.

Uncompressed:
"We need support for homeless families"

Compressed:
"We need 72 wool blankets, 60x80 inches, delivered by October 15th, plus 72 sleeping pads and hygiene kits"

The compressed version is actionable. The uncompressed version is noise.

Why AI Fails at Compression

Most AI does the opposite. It expands.

You ask: "How do I solve my problem?"

They give you: 5 options, each with 3 sub-points, all hedged with "it depends"

That's information overload, not compression.

The Skill

Compression requires:

  1. Understanding the context deeply
  2. Knowing what can be ignored
  3. Identifying the binding constraint
  4. Translating that into action

This is a human skill. No AI can do it alone.

In-Kind Giving as a Model for Usefulness

Generic Approach:
Nonprofit says "we need money" → Donor gives $10,000 → Nonprofit figures out what to buy → If it doesn't work, they wasted money

In-Kind Approach:
Nonprofit says "we need 500 meals" → Donor provides 500 meals → Problem is solved → Everyone knows if it worked

Why In-Kind Works

  • Specificity — It's concrete, not vague
  • Accountability — You can see the impact
  • Efficiency — No translation layer, no waste
  • Knowledge respect — The recipient's expertise is directly useful
  • Verification — Both parties can verify the outcome

The Lesson for AI

Most AI fails at this. It tries to be the expert.

But you're the expert on your situation. What you need is someone who respects your judgment and helps you execute it.

That's the in-kind model applied to assistance.

Building Systems That Matter

Systems That Matter

  • ✓ Solve a specific problem for a specific person
  • ✓ Are hard to copy (require understanding)
  • ✓ Create new possibilities (don't just patch)
  • ✓ Compound over time (each use adds value)
  • ✓ Align with reality (not metrics)

Systems That Don't Matter

  • ✗ Solve vague problems for everyone
  • ✗ Are easy to copy (generic)
  • ✗ Maintain status quo
  • ✗ Decay over time
  • ✗ Optimize for metrics
OpenClaw Labs
What we could have built: "A learning platform where students answer questions."

What we built: "A knowledge creation system where people propose explanations for unsolved problems, get criticized by AI, refine their thinking, and contribute to humanity's understanding."

Notice the difference.

Measuring Real Impact

The Trap

You can measure what's easy to measure, but that usually isn't what matters.

Easy to Measure (Usually Wrong)

  • Page views
  • Time spent
  • Engagement
  • Users
  • Shares

Hard to Measure (Actually Matters)

  • Did this actually help?
  • Did it solve the problem?
  • Did it create new knowledge?
  • Did it make someone more capable?
  • Did it create new possibilities?

The Right Metrics for Usefulness

  1. Problem solved? Did the output actually help? Can you verify it?
  2. Capability increased? Is the user more capable than before?
  3. Compounding? Does each iteration become easier/better?
  4. Specific outcome? Can you point to something concrete that changed?

Knowledge Markets: The Future

What Is a Knowledge Market?

A system where:

  • Unsolved problems are transparent
  • Current best thinking is documented
  • Gaps are explicit
  • Anyone can propose improvements
  • Good ideas are tested and voted on
  • Knowledge compounds

Why This is the Future

Right now, useful knowledge is:

  • Hidden in research papers
  • Locked in people's heads
  • Discussed in closed forums
  • Lost when people leave
  • Siloed across organizations

A knowledge market makes it liquid. It flows. It compounds.

And unlike platforms, it actually solves problems instead of engaging users.

OpenClaw Labs as Example

Unsolved problems → Current best thinking → Gaps identified → Proposals submitted → AI criticism → Refinement → Voting → Knowledge library → Compounding

Your Role: Build Usefulness, Not Engagement

You Have a Choice

Option 1: Build What Engages

  • Optimize for metrics
  • Make things feel good
  • Maximize usage
  • Follow the incentives

Result: Success, but empty.

Option 2: Build What's Useful

  • Optimize for outcomes
  • Make things actually work
  • Maximize impact
  • Question the incentives

Result: Harder, but meaningful.

"The future isn't better engagement. The future is transparent problems, better thinking, and knowledge at scale. And it's only possible if we build for usefulness, not engagement."

Questions for You

Reflection Exercise

  1. What's my specific knowledge? (What do I know from curiosity, not training?)
  2. What problem am I uniquely positioned to solve?
  3. Who would benefit most from my help?
  4. What would it look like to build something genuinely useful for them?
  5. How would I measure success? (Outcome, not engagement)
  6. What am I willing to give up? (Metrics, approval, scale)
  7. Do I have the courage to be specific instead of vague?
  8. Am I ready to build knowledge infrastructure instead of a platform?

Answer honestly.
